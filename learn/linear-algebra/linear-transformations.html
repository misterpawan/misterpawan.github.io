Linear Transformations

Chapter 2 • Section 2-6

"Functions, but for vectors! üîÑ"

‚ú® The Definition

A transformation $T: \RR^n \to \RR^m$ is linear if it satisfies two golden rules for all vectors $\vec{x}, \vec{y}$ and scalars $a$:

1 $T(\vec{x} + \vec{y}) = T(\vec{x}) + T(\vec{y})$ (Preserves Addition)
2 $T(a\vec{x}) = aT(\vec{x})$ (Preserves Scalar Mult.)

Key Insight

Every matrix transformation is linear. And amazingly, every linear transformation is a matrix transformation!

"Linear" = "Matrix"

Finding the Matrix of $T$

If $T$ is linear, it is induced by a unique matrix $A$. How do we find it?

The Recipe

  1. Take the standard basis vectors $\vec{e}_1, \vec{e}_2, \dots, \vec{e}_n$ (columns of $I$).
  2. See where $T$ sends them: compute $T(\vec{e}_1), T(\vec{e}_2), \dots$.
  3. These become the columns of your matrix $A$.
$$ A = \begin{bmatrix} T(\vec{e}_1) & T(\vec{e}_2) & \cdots & T(\vec{e}_n) \end{bmatrix} $$

üîÑ Rotations in $\RR^2$

A rotation $R_\theta$ is a linear transformation! Its matrix is determined by rotating $\vec{e}_1$ and $\vec{e}_2$.

$$ A = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix} $$

Reflections & Composition

Reflections

Reflection across a line is also linear.

  • x-axis: $\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}$
  • y-axis: $\begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$
  • $y=x$: $\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$

Composition

Doing $T$ then $S$ ($S \circ T$) corresponds to matrix multiplication $AB$.

Note: $S \circ T$ means $T$ happens first, so we multiply $B$ (for $T$) by $A$ (for $S$) on the left: $AB$.
