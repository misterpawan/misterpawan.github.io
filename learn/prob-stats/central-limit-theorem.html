Central Limit Theorem

The most important theorem in statistics. Why everything tends to become Normal.

1 The Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

Let \( X_1, X_2, \dots, X_n \) be i.i.d. random variables with mean \( \mu \) and variance \( \sigma^2 < \infty \).
Then, as \( n \to \infty \), the standardized sample mean converges in distribution to the Standard Normal Distribution \( N(0,1) \).

$$ Z_n = \frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} \xrightarrow{d} N(0,1) $$

Key Insight: It does NOT matter what the original distribution of \( X_i \) is (as long as variance is finite). The sum (or average) will eventually look like a Bell Curve.

2 CLT in Action

Choose a distribution (Uniform or Exponential). We will take samples of size \( n \), calculate their mean, and plot the histogram of these means.
Notice how the shape becomes "Bell-shaped" as \( n \) increases.

3 Normal Approximation

Because of CLT, we can approximate probabilities for sums of random variables using the Normal distribution.

Binomial Approximation

If \( X \sim \text{Binomial}(n,p) \), then \( X \) is a sum of \( n \) Bernoulli trials.
For large \( n \), \( X \approx N(np, np(1-p)) \).
