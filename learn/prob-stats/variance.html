Variance and Standard Deviation

Understanding variability and spread in data.

Motivation

The expected value \(E[X]\) (or mean) gives us the "center" of a distribution, but it doesn't tell us how spread out the data is. Consider three scenarios where you win money:

  • Case A: You get $1000 guaranteed.
  • Case B: Coin toss: Heads you get $2000, Tails you get $0.
  • Case C: Lottery: 1/1000 chance to win $1,000,000, otherwise $0.

In all three cases, the expected winning is $1000. However, the risk or variability is vastly different. Variance measures this spread.

Definition

Variance

Let \(\mu = E[X]\). The variance of \(X\), denoted as \(\text{Var}(X)\) or \(\sigma^2\), is: $$ \text{Var}(X) = E[(X - \mu)^2] = \sum_x (x - \mu)^2 p(x) $$

Standard Deviation

The standard deviation is the square root of the variance: $$ \sigma_X = \sqrt{\text{Var}(X)} $$

Computational Formula

There is often a more convenient way to calculate variance:

$$ \text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2 $$

Proof:
\begin{align*} \text{Var}(X) &= E[(X - \mu)^2] \\ &= E[X^2 - 2\mu X + \mu^2] \\ &= E[X^2] - 2\mu E[X] + \mu^2 \\ &= E[X^2] - 2\mu^2 + \mu^2 \\ &= E[X^2] - \mu^2 \end{align*}

Properties

Scaling

$$ \text{Var}(aX) = a^2 \text{Var}(X) $$ Variance scales by the square of the constant.

Shifting

$$ \text{Var}(X + b) = \text{Var}(X) $$ Adding a constant shifts the distribution but does not change its spread.

Example: Variance of Binomial

For a Binomial random variable \(X \sim \text{Binomial}(n, p)\):

  • Mean: \( E[X] = np \)
  • Variance: \( \text{Var}(X) = np(1-p) \)
