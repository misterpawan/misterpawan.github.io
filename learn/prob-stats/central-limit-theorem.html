Central Limit Theorem

The magic of statistics. Why the Bell Curve is everywhere.

1. The Theorem

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) states that if you take the sum (or average) of a large number of independent random variables, the result will look like a Normal Distribution, regardless of the original distribution!

$$ Z_n = \frac{\overline{X}_n - \mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} \to \mathcal{N}(0,1) $$

2. CLT Playground

Choose a "Source Distribution" that looks nothing like a Bell Curve (e.g., Uniform or Exponential). Then, increase the "Sample Size" (\( n \)).
We will simulate summing \( n \) numbers from that source, repeat it 1000 times, and plot the histogram of the sums.

3. Why it works

When you add random variables, their probability distributions convolve. Convolution acts like a smoothing filter. Repeated convolution smooths out any sharp edges or skewness, eventually converging to the smoothest possible shape: the Gaussian (Normal) curve.
