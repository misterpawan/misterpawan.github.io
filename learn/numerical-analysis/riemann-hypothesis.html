Bernhard Riemann

1826 ‚Äî 1866

In 1859, German mathematician Bernhard Riemann published a groundbreaking 8-page paper: "On the Number of Primes Less Than a Given Magnitude".

He wasn't trying to be famous; he just wanted to count prime numbers. In doing so, he connected the discrete world of primes integers to the continuous world of complex analysis.

The Origin

Riemann took Euler's zeta function (defined for real numbers) and extended it to the complex plane ($s = \sigma + it$).

"...it is very probable that all roots are real. Certainly one would wish for a stricter proof here; I have meanwhile temporarily put aside the search for this..."

The Zeta Function

Defined for complex numbers $s$. It encodes information about prime numbers via the Euler Product:

$$ \zeta(s) = \prod_{p} \frac{1}{1-p^{-s}} $$

The Zeros

The function equals zero in two places:

  • Trivial Zeros: Negative even integers (-2, -4, -6...). Easy to find.
  • Non-Trivial Zeros: The mystery. We know they lie in the "Critical Strip" where $0 < Re(s) < 1$.

The Hypothesis

Riemann guessed that ALL non-trivial zeros lie exactly on the vertical line:

$$ Re(s) = \frac{1}{2} $$

History of Progress

1900

Hilbert's Problems

David Hilbert lists RH as the 8th problem in his famous list of 23 unsolved problems for the 20th century.

1914

Hardy's Theorem

G.H. Hardy proves that infinitely many zeros lie on the critical line. (But are they ALL there?)

2004

Computational Verification

Xavier Gourdon verifies the first 10 trillion zeros lie on the critical line. Still not a proof!

Today

Clay Millennium Prize

The Clay Mathematics Institute offers $1,000,000 for a correct proof (or disproof).

Prime Number Distribution

The distribution of prime numbers $\pi(x)$ would be known with incredible precision.

$$ |\pi(x) - \text{Li}(x)| < \frac{1}{8\pi} \sqrt{x} \ln(x) $$

Basically, the "randomness" of prime numbers is actually extremely structured, controlled by these zeros.

Cryptography & Algorithms

Many algorithms in number theory rely on the Generalized Riemann Hypothesis (GRH) to guarantee speed.

  • Miller-Rabin Primality Test: Becomes fully deterministic (not just probabilistic).
  • RSA Encryption: While RH doesn't break RSA directly, deeper knowledge of primes could lead to better factoring algorithms.
