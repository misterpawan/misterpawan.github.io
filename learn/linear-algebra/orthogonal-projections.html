üìê Orthogonality Basics

Orthogonal Vectors

Two vectors $\vec{u}$ and $\vec{v}$ are orthogonal if their dot product is zero:

$\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = 0$

Orthonormal Set

A set of vectors is orthonormal if they are all orthogonal to each other AND have length 1.

$\vec{u}_i \cdot \vec{u}_j = \delta_{ij}$

Orthogonal Complement $U^\perp$

The set of all vectors orthogonal to every vector in a subspace $U$.

$$ U^\perp = \{ \vec{x} \in V \mid \vec{x} \cdot \vec{u} = 0 \text{ for all } \vec{u} \in U \} $$

üìú Fundamental Lemmas

Independent Lemma

Let $S = \{\vec{x}_1, \dots, \vec{x}_k\}$ be an independent set in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

If $\vec{u} \notin \text{Span}(S)$, then $S \cup \{\vec{u}\}$ is also independent.

Orthogonal Lemma

Let $\{\vec{f}_1, \dots, \vec{f}_k\}$ be an orthogonal set of non-zero vectors.

Then the set is linearly independent.

Proof Idea

Assume $c_1\vec{f}_1 + \dots + c_k\vec{f}_k = \vec{0}$. Dot product with $\vec{f}_i$ gives $c_i \|\vec{f}_i\|^2 = 0$, so $c_i = 0$.

üî® The Gram-Schmidt Algorithm

An algorithm to construct an orthogonal basis $\{\vec{f}_1, \dots, \vec{f}_k\}$ from any basis $\{\vec{x}_1, \dots, \vec{x}_k\}$.

1
$\vec{f}_1 = \vec{x}_1$
2
$\vec{f}_2 = \vec{x}_2 - \frac{\vec{x}_2 \cdot \vec{f}_1}{\|\vec{f}_1\|^2}\vec{f}_1$
3
$\vec{f}_3 = \vec{x}_3 - \frac{\vec{x}_3 \cdot \vec{f}_1}{\|\vec{f}_1\|^2}\vec{f}_1 - \frac{\vec{x}_3 \cdot \vec{f}_2}{\|\vec{f}_2\|^2}\vec{f}_2$
...
k
$\vec{f}_k = \vec{x}_k - \text{proj}_{U_{k-1}}(\vec{x}_k)$

Convert a basis $\{\vec{x}_1, \vec{x}_2\}$ into an orthogonal basis $\{\vec{f}_1, \vec{f}_2\}$.
Formula: $\vec{f}_1 = \vec{x}_1$, $\vec{f}_2 = \vec{x}_2 - \text{proj}_{\vec{f}_1}(\vec{x}_2)$.

f1 = (4, 0)
f2 = (0, 3)

Orthogonal Projection

The projection of $\vec{x}$ onto a subspace $U$ is the "shadow" of $\vec{x}$ on $U$.

$$ \proj_U(\vec{x}) = \sum_{i=1}^k \frac{\vec{x} \cdot \vec{f}_i}{\|\vec{f}_i\|^2} \vec{f}_i $$

Where $\{\vec{f}_1, \dots, \vec{f}_k\}$ is an orthogonal basis for $U$.

üéØ The Projection Theorem

Let $U$ be a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^n$. Then every vector $\vec{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ can be written uniquely as:

$$ \vec{x} = \vec{p} + \vec{z} $$
  • $\vec{p} = \text{proj}_U(\vec{x})$ is in $U$.
  • $\vec{z} = \vec{x} - \vec{p}$ is in $U^\perp$ (orthogonal to $U$).

Best Approximation Property

The projection $\vec{p}$ is the vector in $U$ that is closest to $\vec{x}$.

$\|\vec{x} - \vec{p}\| < \|\vec{x} - \vec{u}\|$ for all other $\vec{u} \in U$
